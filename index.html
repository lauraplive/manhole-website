Religious to Residential: The Business Behind Flipping Churches into Condos

More manholes caught fire this past winter than in the last five years. A new analysis shows the close link with road salt and harsh winters — and that some neighborhoods suffer far more than others.

By Laura Pellicer and Ambra Schuster

May 20, 2026

On a Saturday morning well before sunrise, psychotherapist Siena Vaccara, 27, woke to a thundering boom outside of her apartment in Brooklyn’s Chinatown on 8th Avenue.

“ It was the kind of explosion that was so loud you, you didn't even wake up and gasp,” Vaccara said. “ You were immediately feet on the floor standing up.”

A red haze glowed outside her second-floor window and the smell of smoke started to creep into her apartment. At the corner of her street, a manhole was ablaze. Flames whipped against the rim of the manhole while a thick gray cloud. Before Vaccara could process what was happening, a second explosion went off.  Now, the smoke was thick.

“It was really scary because although we had the windows closed, the apartment was filling up with smoke, and it was hard to breathe,” Vaccara said.

She thought about her neighbors. On her busy stretch of 8th Avenue, there are outdoor markets, a fruit stand, a Buddhist temple and streams of pedestrians. The timing had been lucky.

“If that happened during the day, that would've been a tragedy.”

Vacarra’s experience is far from isolated. New York City sees thousands of manhole fires every year, according to FDNY dispatch data.

More than 5,200 manhole fires were recorded in the current fiscal year* alone – which includes last winter. That’s the highest number of fires and explosions since 2021 and more than triple the 1,539 incidents logged in 2023, when fires dipped to a near-decade low.

Shrinking membership, and therefore shrinking revenue, combined with rising operational and maintenance costs for “too many churches” forces hard decisions when buildings no longer serve their mission, said Kienzle. “We’re sitting on assets that could do good in the neighborhood. But it’s all locked up in decrepit buildings.”

Proceeds from these church sales are often dedicated for “mission funds” that support more promising ministries elsewhere.
